About DHQ

Mission

Welcome to Digital Humanities Quarterly (DHQ), an open-access, peer-reviewed, digital journal covering all aspects of digital media in the humanities. Published by the Association for Computers and the Humanities (ACH) and the Alliance of Digital Humanities Organizations (ADHO), DHQ is also a community experiment in journal publication, with a commitment to:

  • experimenting with publication formats and the rhetoric of digital authoring
  • using open standards to deliver journal content
  • developing translation services and multilingual reviewing in keeping with the global nature of digital humanities research

DHQ publishes a wide range of peer-reviewed materials, including:

  • Scholarly articles, case studies, and field reports
  • Experiments in interactive media
  • Reviews of books, web sites, new media art installations, digital humanities systems and tools

You can find answers to frequently asked questions about the journal on our FAQ page. Please contact us for further information.

Scope of the Journal

Digital humanities is a diverse and evolving field that encompasses the practice of humanities research in and through information technology, and the exploration of how the humanities may evolve through their engagement with technology, media, and computational methods. DHQ seeks to provide an interdisciplinary forum where practitioners, theorists, researchers, and teachers in this field can share their work with each other and with those from related disciplines.

DHQ seeks to publish articles that enable researchers and practitioners in different subfields of digital humanities to learn from one another. As a result, our focus is primarily on articles that focus critical attention on their own methods and approaches, and that communicate clearly and engagingly about the goals, context, situational factors, design considerations, and other factors that may help a reader apply the research to their own work and understand its relevance. Routine applications of well-established tools, and highly specialized articles that do not articulate their broader relevance, are less likely to be a good fit.

DHQ does not typically publish the following:

  • Bibliometric studies and literature reviews
  • Opinion pieces and articles that are purely speculative
  • Articles that describe a routine application of a well-understood tool or method to a specific humanities research task
  • Articles that are primarily focused on literary or historical interpretation, with only brief or superficial reference to digital tools and methods.

If you're unsure of whether a prospective submission falls within DHQ's rubric, please contact the editors.
